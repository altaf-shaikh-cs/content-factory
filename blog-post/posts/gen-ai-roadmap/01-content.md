# I Almost Put Everything I Know About GenAI Into One Roadmap. I Didn't.

Open any "Generative AI Roadmap" post and you'll see the same thing: a wall of boxes. Prompting, embeddings, agents, fine-tuning, vector databases, RAG, evals, MCP, whatever shipped last week. Forty nodes deep, arrows everywhere, and a little voice in your head asking: am I even supposed to know all of this yet?

Most people close the tab right there. Not because they're lazy. Because the roadmap didn't answer the one question they actually came with — "where do I stand, and what's next" — it just made them feel further behind.

That's the trap. A roadmap's job isn't to prove how much there is to know. It's to tell you what you can safely ignore for now.

So when I sat down to put together a roadmap for people getting started with GenAI, I made myself a rule: only the fundamentals that aren't going to change next month. No tool-of-the-week entries. No "also learn this" side branches. If a topic was trending but not foundational, it stayed out — even when it was tempting to add it, because it felt like it made the roadmap look more complete.

The result is smaller than I wanted it to be, honestly. Every time I added a new box, I asked myself: is this something a beginner needs to be functional with GenAI, or is this something I just think is cool right now? Most of the time the honest answer was the second one, and the box came back out.

That's the real work of building a roadmap like this — not adding things, but having the discipline to leave them out. It's easier to write a big list than a short, correct one.

I'm calling this Level 1 on purpose. It's not the full picture — it's the floor. Once someone can walk through it and honestly say "yes, yes, yes, no" instead of "no, no, no, no," they're ready for the next layer. That advanced roadmap is coming later. This one isn't trying to be it.

What makes a roadmap like this different:

**It's a checklist, not a syllabus.** You're not meant to study it top to bottom in order. You're meant to walk through it and mark what you already have.

**It only holds what won't be outdated by next quarter.** No specific model names, no tool that might get replaced in six months. Just the fundamentals under all of that.

**It's deliberately incomplete.** Anything that felt like a "nice to have" got cut, even when it was hard to cut. Overloading a beginner roadmap doesn't make someone more prepared — it just makes them close the tab.

**It's meant to be validated against, not memorized.** The value isn't in reading it once. It's in coming back to it in a month and seeing which boxes finally turned green.

When to actually use something like this:

- You're starting from zero and don't know what order to learn things in
- You've been learning GenAI stuff for a while but never checked if you're missing something basic
- You're about to jump into advanced material and want to make sure the floor is solid first

How to use it: open the roadmap, go node by node, and be honest with yourself about each one. Don't skim it looking for validation — actually check whether you could explain each item to someone else, not just recognize the name. The gaps you find are more useful than the boxes you tick.

Here's the one thing to do right now: open the roadmap, find the single node you're least confident about, and spend today on just that one. Not all of it. One box.

I'd genuinely like to know how this lands. Where did you already know everything? Where did a box surprise you? And if you've been through something like this before — what did your version leave out that mine didn't? If this was useful, tell me, and I'll get moving on the advanced version sooner.

This one's about the mindset before the tools — knowing what you actually need to know before you go chase what's shiny. The next posts in this series get into the tools themselves.

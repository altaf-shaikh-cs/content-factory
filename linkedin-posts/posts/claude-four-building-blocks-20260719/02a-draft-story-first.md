Imagine you just hired someone brilliant. Sharp, fast, can research and write anything.

Day one, they're useless.

Not because they lack skill. Because they don't know your formatting, your templates, how your team organizes work, or what "done" looks like here.

That's Claude, every time you open a fresh chat.

A consultant wrote up the four things that fix this, after getting the same question a dozen times from firm leaders: "What's the difference between Skills, Plugins, Projects, and Artifacts, and when do I use each?"

The four map cleanly to onboarding a new hire:

Skills are the memos you hand them. "Here's how we format things. Here's our checklist." Written once in plain markdown, picked up automatically, persists across every conversation.

Plugins are the full toolkit. Skills bundled with connectors, commands, and sub-agents into one installable package. Install it and Claude shows up already knowing how your work runs.

Projects are the walls. One workspace per matter, files and history isolated. Nothing bleeds from one engagement into the next.

Artifacts are the actual work product. Documents, decks, spreadsheets, small apps. Files you can ship, not text in a chat window.

Skills give consistency. Plugins give capability. Projects give separation. Artifacts give output.

Configure them, and Claude stops being a chatbot and becomes a work environment. Skip them, and you hired the brilliant new person and never onboarded them.

Which of the four are you actually using?

A viral article with 18 million views just described, step for step, the system I quietly built for myself over the last two months.

It's about loops.

I used to drive my AI agent one prompt at a time. Type, wait, review, fix, ask again. I was the engine. The AI was just the tool in my hand.

Then I flipped it. I gave the agent a goal, a way to check its own work, and a rule for when to stop.

That's a loop. And the article nailed why most loops fail. They're missing three organs:

A verifier. Without a real check, the agent just agrees with itself on repeat. The model that did the work is far too generous a grader of its own homework.

State. A real loop keeps a small record: what's done, what failed, what's next. Tomorrow's run resumes instead of starting from zero.

A stop condition. Success, or a hard limit. Skip this and you built a machine that runs all night for nothing.

My version: a content pipeline that runs on a schedule, drafts posts from my idea backlog, passes them through a separate reviewer agent, and tracks its own state in a plain markdown file. I review the output, not the process.

The article's build order matches what I learned the hard way: get one manual run reliable, save it as a skill, wrap it in a loop, and only then schedule it. Never schedule what you haven't proven by hand.
